Meet our Team


Ian Gotlib, Ph.D. - Principal Investigator

Dr. Gotlib runs the Stanford Mood and Anxiety Disorders Lab. In his research, Dr. Gotlib is examining psychological and biological factors that place individuals at increased risk for depression, as well as processes that are involved in recovery from this disorder. He is conducting research examining cognitive, social, and endocrinological factors and genetics in depressed individuals, patterns of brain function and structure that characterize depressed and anxious adults and children, and predictors of depression in children at familial risk for developing this disorder. Dr. Gotlib is also examining the impact of innovative cognitive and neural procedures both to treat depression and to reduce young childrenâ€™s risk of developing depression.

Tiffany Ho, Ph.D. - Postdoctoral Fellow

I am a postdoctoral fellow in Dr. Ian Gotlib's Stanford Mood and Anxiety Disorders Lab, and received my Ph.D. in cognitive psychology/neuroscience from UC San Diego in 2012. Prior to joining the Gotlib lab, I was a postdoctoral researcher at UC San Francisco, where I used functional neuroimaging approaches to understand adolescent depression and suicidality. At Stanford, I will be combining structural and functional neuroimaging methods to better understand neural risk mechanisms for depression and suicidal behaviors. I am interested in developing brain-based models of psychopathology, with the goal that this work will inform current prevention and treatment efforts.

Lucy King - Ph.D. Student

I am a Ph.D. student in the Stanford Mood and & Anxiety Disorders Laboratory and I am assisting on protocol development, training, and data analysis for the BABIES study. I am broadly interested in how the early environment affects long-term mental health and resilience. My research focuses on the development of stress reactivity and regulation in children exposed to early life stress, as well as factors that foster wellbeing, such as positive parenting. In addition, I am focused on improving how scientists measure positive and negative experiences in early life. Before arriving at Stanford, I worked on studies of mental health following childhood exposure to disasters and the effects of perinatal stress and parenting on biological reactivity and regulation in 6-month-old infants.
